## Objectives
- Import media more intentionally instead of just dragging everything in and hoping for the best.
- Use events, keywords, ratings, and roles to make footage easier to find later.
- Build habits that reduce hunting, duplicate imports, and clip confusion.

## Lesson
One of the easiest ways to level up in Final Cut Pro is to stop treating importing as a boring prelude and start treating it as the first real editorial decision.

Apple’s importing and media-management guides make this clear: import is not just file transfer. It is where you decide how clips enter the project, where they live, how they are organized, whether they are analyzed or transcoded, and how searchable they will be later.

### Import with intention

During import, ask:

- Which event should this go into?
- Do I want to create optimized or proxy media?
- Do I want analysis or keyword organization now?
- Am I importing from the right source location?

Those questions matter because bad imports create slow editing later.

### Events are your first sorting layer

If your library is the bigger container, events are your first meaningful grouping system. Good event-level organization means you can quickly separate:

- interview footage
- B-roll
- drone shots
- screen recordings
- stills
- music or effects

This is more useful than one giant pile of clips in one event.

### Keywords and ratings are not overkill

Beginners often skip keywording because it feels like “extra admin.” Then they end up skimming the same footage again and again because they cannot find what matters.

Keywords and ratings are worth using when:

- you have lots of clips
- you want to flag favorites
- you need to group clips by subject, location, speaker, or use case

Examples:

- `close-up`
- `wide`
- `good reaction`
- `usable dialogue`
- `establishing shot`

These do not need to be elaborate. They just need to make future selection faster.

### Roles are more useful than they first appear

Apple notes that imported clips get default roles such as:

- Video
- Titles
- Dialogue
- Music
- Effects

That may sound technical, but roles become very helpful later for:

- cleaner audio organization
- timeline filtering
- better export and mix clarity

You do not need to obsess over them on day one. But you should know they exist and that they are part of a more professional workflow.

### A good beginner import habit

Try this order:

1. create or choose the right event
2. import from a known source
3. name or keyword anything that will be hard to find later
4. rate favorites or rejects if the footage set is large
5. only then start rough cutting

That keeps your browser usable.

> [!TIP]
> Every minute you spend making source footage findable can save multiple minutes of future re-skimming.

### Level-up question

When you cannot find a shot later, ask whether the problem was:

- weak naming
- weak event structure
- no keywords
- no ratings
- or simple impatience during import

That question teaches you more than just “I should have been more organized.”

## Checklist
- Import one batch of footage into a deliberately chosen event.
- Add at least three useful keywords or one meaningful rating pass.
- Identify where roles could help keep audio or titles clearer later.
- Write one import habit you want to repeat on future projects.

## Reflection
- When you think about your own editing pain, is the bigger issue finding the right clip or knowing what to do once you find it?
- Which kind of organization would save you the most time next week: better event separation, ratings, keywords, or roles?
