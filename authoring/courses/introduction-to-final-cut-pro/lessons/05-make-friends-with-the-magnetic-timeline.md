## Objectives
- Understand the primary storyline, connected clips, and storylines.
- Learn why the magnetic timeline behaves differently from a track-based editor.
- Reduce fear and surprise when clips ripple, attach, or stay in sync.

## Lesson
If Final Cut Pro has one concept that separates it most clearly from many other editing apps, it is the **magnetic timeline**.

This is the point where some users either start to love Final Cut or keep fighting it forever.

### The primary storyline

Apple’s timeline documentation describes the primary storyline as the main sequence of clips in the timeline. Think of it as the backbone of your edit. It is where the main flow of the story lives.

That backbone matters because Final Cut Pro tries to protect sync and timing relationships around it.

### Connected clips

Connected clips attach to the primary storyline. Apple’s documentation calls out the common uses clearly:

- cutaways
- composited visuals
- titles
- sound effects
- music elements

The key point is that connected clips remain attached and synced unless you deliberately move or remove them.

That is one of the strengths of the magnetic timeline. Instead of constantly repairing track chaos, you can let the app preserve relationships for you.

### Why the timeline feels “magnetic”

It is called magnetic because clips ripple and close gaps in ways that maintain continuity. For beginners, that can feel either amazing or annoying, depending on whether you understand what just happened.

A healthier way to think about it:

- Final Cut is trying to help maintain story continuity
- it is not trying to emulate old-school track behavior by default

### What the magnetic timeline is great at

| Strength | Why it helps |
| --- | --- |
| Keeping connected material in sync | Less accidental drift between cutaways, sound, and the main sequence |
| Closing gaps intelligently | Less timeline clutter during rough cutting |
| Encouraging story-first editing | The primary storyline stays visually central |

### What trips beginners up

| Friction point | Why it happens |
| --- | --- |
| “My clips moved!” | You made a ripple-style change and the timeline preserved continuity |
| “Why is this attached here?” | A connected clip is linked to a point in the primary storyline |
| “Why can’t I just leave a hole there?” | Final Cut prefers continuity unless you deliberately use a gap or alternate tool |

### Storylines help tame complexity

A sequence of connected clips can become a storyline. This is useful when a bunch of connected material should behave together as a unit.

Examples:

- a stack of cutaways
- a title sequence
- layered sound design pieces

This helps the magnetic timeline stay readable instead of becoming a floating cloud of fragments.

### A better way to practice

Open a small project and intentionally do this:

1. add a few clips to the primary storyline
2. connect a title or cutaway
3. move or trim the main clip
4. watch what stays attached

That exercise teaches the system much faster than reading about it abstractly.

> [!TIP]
> When the magnetic timeline surprises you, ask what relationship Final Cut Pro was trying to preserve. That question usually explains the behavior.

The more you stop expecting tracks and start understanding relationships, the more Final Cut begins to feel smart instead of stubborn.

## Checklist
- Build a tiny primary storyline with three or four clips.
- Add at least one connected clip above or below it.
- Move a primary-storyline clip and watch what stays attached.
- Group connected clips into a storyline if the project has enough material to try it.

## Reflection
- Does the magnetic timeline currently feel more protective or more annoying to you?
- Which is more likely in your own work: losing sync in a track-based mindset, or feeling frustrated by Final Cut’s attempts to preserve relationships?
