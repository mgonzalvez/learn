## Objectives
- Learn how to test this kind of game efficiently without getting lost in a sea of opinions.
- Develop a practical anti-clone discipline so your game learns from benchmarks without becoming one.
- Leave with concrete next steps for the next four to six weeks of design work.

## Lesson
Now that you have a first playable, your work changes shape. The question is no longer "Can I imagine a good game?" It becomes "What evidence do I need, and what should I change next?"

### How to playtest this genre well

Worker placement and tableau games can hide problems for several sessions because players enjoy exploring new cards and spaces even when the underlying structure is shaky. That is why your tests should be targeted.

Good first-test questions:

- Are two or three spaces obviously more important than the rest?
- Does the tableau meaningfully change priorities by turn three or four?
- Is the solo pressure too passive, too mean, or too admin-heavy?
- Does cleanup or mitigation produce interesting tension?
- Is one engine line clearly more flexible than the others?
- Do the crisis or event triggers arrive early enough to matter but late enough to feel earned?

Strong practice from design literature says to enter each test with one to three explicit questions, track the data that answers them, and write down problems separately from proposed fixes. That discipline matters even more in a medium-weight design because there are many tempting levers to pull.

### How not to accidentally clone your benchmarks

This matters. If you love The Manhattan Project family, you can drift into derivative design without noticing.

Here is a healthy anti-clone checklist:

### Do not copy the action menu one-to-one

If your board has obvious equivalents of the benchmark spaces in the same relationships, you are too close.

### Do not copy the emotional arc

Ask whether your game creates its own rhythm. Is your pressure system distinct? Is your scoring story different? Is your solo tension distinctive?

### Do not copy signature subsystems unless they are transformed

If you borrow:

- asynchronous retrieval
- occupied-space surcharges
- pollution pressure
- espionage use of opponents' tableaus
- multi-part solo win conditions

then ask what new design job that borrowed subsystem is doing in *your* game.

### Do copy underlying design principles

Copying principles is healthy:

- make placements consequential
- give the tableau identity
- let pressure systems matter
- create multiple viable plans
- reward timing, not only accumulation

That is design literacy, not theft.

> [!TIP]
> If someone described your game in one sentence and it sounded like "Energy Empire, but with..." you probably need another round of structural differentiation.

### Your next four to six weeks

Here is a practical roadmap:

| Week | Focus |
| --- | --- |
| 1 | Build and solo-test the first playable by yourself 3 to 5 times |
| 2 | Run 2 focused solo revisions and 1 multiplayer sanity test |
| 3 | Track which action spaces and engine lines are underused or dominant |
| 4 | Cut one weak subsystem and sharpen one promising subsystem |
| 5 | Rewrite the rulebook draft and test from the rulebook if possible, especially the solo setup and win conditions |
| 6 | Decide whether the design is becoming more itself or more derivative |

If you follow that with discipline, you will learn far more than by endlessly adding cards.

### A final creative standard

The goal is not to create "a game that is kind of like some games I enjoy." The goal is to create a game whose mechanisms reinforce a distinct player fantasy and pressure profile:

- *why* does your board fight back?
- *why* does your tableau feel worth building?
- *why* does solo play feel purposeful?
- *why* does this design deserve to exist next to the games you admire?

Those are grown-up design questions. They are also the fun ones.

## Checklist
- Write the 3 specific questions for your first real prototype tests.
- List the 2 benchmark features you are intentionally *not* copying directly.
- Write one sentence explaining what makes your game's pressure profile distinct.
- Schedule the next 3 tests you want to run: solo, multiplayer, and rules-teach.

## Reflection
- What part of your current design feels most original already, and what part still leans too heavily on your benchmark inspirations?
- If a smart player finished your prototype and said "this has a real identity," what would you most hope they were responding to?

## Quiz
### Question 1
What is the healthiest way to avoid accidentally cloning a benchmark game?
- [ ] Refuse to study benchmark games closely
- [ ] Change the theme and keep the systems the same
- [x] Borrow principles and pressures while making sure your core rhythm and subsystem jobs are genuinely your own
- [ ] Add more complexity until the similarities are hidden
Explanation: Originality usually comes from a distinct structure and pressure profile, not from cosmetic changes or unnecessary complication.

### Question 2
Why should your first playtests use explicit questions?
- [ ] Because otherwise people will not have fun
- [ ] Because it makes the game seem more professional
- [x] Because targeted questions make feedback and data far more useful
- [ ] Because players should only discuss one part of the game
Explanation: Clear questions turn a session into usable design evidence.

### Question 3
What is a strong sign that your design still needs differentiation?
- [ ] Players understand it quickly
- [x] The easiest short description sounds like "Energy Empire, but..." or "Viticulture with..."
- [ ] The prototype uses worker placement
- [ ] The solo mode has a pressure deck
Explanation: If the game's identity is still mostly comparative, it probably has not found its own structural voice yet.
