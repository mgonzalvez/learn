## Objectives
- Identify common balance and pacing failures in medium-weight worker placement and tableau games.
- Learn a practical framework for testing economy, scoring, tension, and dead turns.
- Define the metrics you want to track in your own prototype.

## Lesson
Balance is not just about making numbers equal. It is about making strategies feel viable, timing feel tense, and outcomes feel earned. In this genre, the most common problems are usually structural before they are numerical.

### What often goes wrong

### 1. The early game is too inert

Players spend too many turns gathering basic resources before the game becomes interesting. This is common when the first build is too expensive or too many prerequisite actions stand between setup and identity.

Fix directions:

- give one early card or board action that accelerates engine identity
- reduce the number of mandatory starter chores
- let first builds matter sooner

### 2. One conversion loop is obviously superior

If one path turns 2 basic resources into points or premium resources more cleanly than the others, experienced players will converge on it quickly.

Fix directions:

- reduce its ceiling
- add setup friction
- strengthen other lines with timing or flexibility advantages
- make its payoffs more vulnerable to contest

### 3. Cleanup or mitigation is always boring

If pollution, maintenance, or repair exists, but players resent interacting with it every game, the design has probably framed a tax instead of a tension source.

Fix directions:

- make cleanup unlock something valuable
- let some cards turn mitigation into engine progress
- ensure neglect is tempting but not dominant

### 4. The tableau becomes runaway

One player's engine reaches lift-off too early and the rest of the game becomes confirmation rather than contest.

Fix directions:

- reduce compounding
- make engine peaks arrive later
- add shared races or scoring windows
- create limited premium opportunities so trailing players can still pivot

### 5. Players have too many nearly equivalent choices

This creates analysis paralysis without excitement.

Fix directions:

- cut redundant spaces
- compress card text
- create clearer strategic identities between lines
- sharpen contested action value

The original Manhattan Project's stronger interaction helps restrain some of these problems because table pressure can disrupt linear engines. Energy Empire demonstrates another route: system pressure through global impacts and pollution. The rulebook makes that especially clear because every time a space on the Global Impact track empties, the game immediately scores an environment row, resets one structure market, and resolves a special global event before play continues. Both games are useful reminders that the design needs a way to push back.

### Balance is easier when you track the right things

From strong playtesting practice, here are the most useful early metrics for this genre:

| Metric | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Turn count or round count | Tells you if engine growth and end conditions are aligned |
| First meaningful build turn | Reveals whether the opening is too slow |
| Final score spread | Shows whether outcomes are razor-thin, healthy, or runaway |
| Pollution / penalty totals | Reveals whether the risk system matters or is ignorable |
| Frequency of blocked-or-taxed key spaces | Shows whether the board is actually producing tension |
| Winning path notes | Reveals whether one line dominates |
| Global event timing | Shows whether the crisis system is just noise or genuinely shaping plans |

If you only ask "Did people have fun?" you will miss too much.

### A practical balance loop

1. Choose one question before a test.
2. Track the numbers that answer that question.
3. Write down the *problem*, not only your guessed solution.
4. Change one or two things, not twelve.
5. Re-test the same question.

Examples:

- "Are players getting their second tableau card too late?"
- "Is cleanup always a desperation move rather than a strategic choice?"
- "Is the specialist-worker line outperforming the generalist line?"

> [!TIP]
> In medium-weight euros, pacing problems are often disguised balance problems, and balance problems are often disguised structural problems.

That means you should resist the urge to change only point values when the real issue is turn tempo, access friction, or option shape.

## Checklist
- Pick 5 metrics you will track in your first prototype tests.
- Write one suspected balance question for each of your three major engine lines.
- Identify one negative-pressure system in your game and decide how to make it strategically interesting.
- Choose one thing you will *not* try to balance numerically until the structure feels stable.

## Reflection
- Which failure worries you more in your own design: runaway engines, weak interaction, or a boring opening?
- If players described your first prototype as "interesting, but the pacing felt off," what do you think would most likely be causing that?

## Quiz
### Question 1
What is a common sign that a pacing problem exists?
- [ ] Players understand the rules quickly
- [ ] The game has multiple scoring paths
- [x] It takes too long before players feel their engine identity actually begins
- [ ] Cleanup actions exist
Explanation: A slow or inert opening often signals that meaningful play starts too late.

### Question 2
Why is it dangerous to treat balance as only a numbers problem?
- [ ] Because numbers never matter in euro games
- [ ] Because players dislike point tracks
- [x] Because dominant strategies often emerge from structure, tempo, access, and compounding, not just point values
- [ ] Because worker placement cannot be balanced
Explanation: Structure often creates the imbalance that numbers merely reveal.

### Question 3
What is the best first response when a mitigation system feels boring?
- [ ] Remove all pressure from the game
- [x] Ask how mitigation could become strategically attractive instead of feeling like a pure tax
- [ ] Double the penalties until players respect it
- [ ] Add more icons to the mitigation cards
Explanation: Negative systems work better when engaging with them can produce meaningful strategic tradeoffs.

### Question 4
What is a strong playtesting habit for balance work?
- [ ] Change many systems after every test
- [ ] Trust your instinct instead of recording numbers
- [ ] Ask only whether people enjoyed the session
- [x] Track a few meaningful metrics tied to a specific design question
Explanation: Measured iteration is much stronger than vague reaction or massive untracked changes.
