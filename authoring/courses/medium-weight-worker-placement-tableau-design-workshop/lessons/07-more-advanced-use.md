## Objectives
- Turn your design notes into a first playable prototype with enough structure to test meaningfully.
- Use a practical scope limit so the game is alive without being overloaded.
- Create concrete component, action, and scoring drafts for your first build.

## Lesson
This is the lesson where you stop admiring systems and start making one. The goal is *not* to produce your final game. The goal is to produce a first playable prototype that is coherent enough to reveal real problems.

For this genre, a strong first playable is usually smaller than you want and more honest than you fear.

### Recommended first-playable scope

Aim for:

- 1 main board
- 3 tableau lanes
- 12 to 18 cards
- 7 to 9 core action spaces
- 1 pressure system
- 1 clear end condition
- 1 solo mode pressure method

Avoid adding at first:

- multiple expansions worth of content
- six resource types
- ten card tags
- a huge event deck
- specialized exceptions on every card

If the first playable already contains all your "cool ideas," testing will become muddy. You want to discover whether the spine works.

### A practical build sequence

### Step 1: Write the player loop

In plain English:

1. place a worker
2. gain or convert resources
3. build or improve part of the tableau
4. manage pressure or race objectives
5. repeat until the end trigger

If you cannot express the player loop clearly, your prototype is not ready.

### Step 2: Choose three engine identities

Example:

- Industrial surge: strong production, high pollution
- Civic efficiency: steadier growth, mitigation and policy rewards
- Science and modernization: better conversions, tighter setup needs

These do not need perfect balance yet. They need to be distinct enough to test.

### Step 3: Define the pressure system

Pick one:

- pollution track
- unrest track
- maintenance demand
- crisis deck
- rival-progress track

The pressure should create decisions, not only punishment.

### Step 4: Draft the scoring model

A solid first-pass scoring model often includes:

- points for built tableau cards
- points for achieved milestones
- points for one or two engine-specific payoffs
- penalty points for neglected pressure

This is enough to see whether the system pulls players toward specialization, diversification, or pure efficiency.

### Step 5: Write a first solo rule in one paragraph

Example:

> At the end of each round, reveal one rival card. It blocks or taxes a key action family, advances one global-pressure track, and claims the leftmost available milestone if eligible.

That is a testable start.

### Concrete concept prompts

If you want ideas, use these as seeds rather than finished pitches:

- **Frontier Power Grid**: build an energy network on a dangerous frontier while balancing industrial growth and ecological backlash.
- **Arcology Council**: place specialists in a vertical city to develop districts, institutions, and public support before crises spiral.
- **Mythic Guildworks**: manage workshops, apprentices, and magical infrastructure while dangerous byproducts accumulate.
- **Harbor Rebuild Authority**: develop trade, civic services, and engineering projects while storms and political pressure keep changing priorities.

Each can support worker placement, tableau lanes, and solo-first pressure naturally.

> [!TIP]
> Your first prototype does not need to prove the game is great. It needs to prove what question you are asking.

## Checklist
- Write your player loop in 5 steps or fewer.
- Choose your 3 tableau lanes and name them.
- Draft 7 to 9 action spaces with one-sentence purposes.
- Pick your pressure system and your end trigger.
- Write a one-paragraph solo rule that creates pressure without lots of upkeep.

## Reflection
- Which of your design ideas feels strong enough to prototype immediately, and which one is probably still "cool in theory" rather than ready?
- If you had to cut your current idea down to the smallest version that still feels exciting, what would remain?

## Quiz
### Question 1
What is the main goal of a first playable prototype?
- [ ] To prove the game is already balanced
- [ ] To impress publishers with final production values
- [x] To reveal whether the core system actually produces the experience you want
- [ ] To include every mechanic you might use later
Explanation: The first playable is a learning tool. Its job is to surface truth quickly.

### Question 2
Why is smaller scope usually better for the first playable?
- [ ] Because medium-weight games should stay simple forever
- [ ] Because solo modes cannot handle bigger systems
- [x] Because tighter scope makes it easier to see whether the spine of the design works
- [ ] Because card effects are not useful early in development
Explanation: A focused prototype reveals problems more clearly than a sprawling one.

### Question 3
What is a strong sign that your design concept is ready to prototype?
- [ ] You have dozens of card ideas
- [ ] You know the final art direction
- [x] You can clearly explain the player loop, pressure system, and engine identity
- [ ] You have already built a second expansion
Explanation: Clarity in the core loop and pressures matters more than content volume.
