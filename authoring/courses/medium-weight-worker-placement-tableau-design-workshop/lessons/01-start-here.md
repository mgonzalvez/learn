## Objectives
- Identify the design traits that make The Manhattan Project and Energy Empire strong study material for your own design work.
- Distinguish between copying a game's surface and learning from its structural strengths.
- Define the target feel for a medium-weight worker placement and tableau-building game you want to build.

## Lesson
The Manhattan Project and The Manhattan Project: Energy Empire are useful anchor games because they are not merely "worker placement plus cards." They each use worker placement to create pressure, then let each player build a personal engine that changes what future turns feel like. That combination is the heart of this course.

The original Manhattan Project is sharper and more confrontational. The rules structure makes players choose between placing workers and retrieving workers, which means tempo is not synchronized. You can keep pressing if you still have labor to place, but that also means you are revealing your priorities and exposing yourself to the table. The game also pushes interaction harder through espionage, air strikes, and the use of opponents' buildings. That creates a design lesson: medium-weight euros become memorable when the action spaces are not the entire game. The personal tableau matters just as much.

Energy Empire keeps the family DNA but changes the emotional profile. Luke Laurie describes it as sharing the main-board-plus-building approach while shifting away from the stronger take-that feel of the original. The actual rulebook makes that shift even clearer. Each turn is either a **Work** turn or a **Generate** turn, and if you Generate, your next turn must be Work. Work turns revolve around placing a Worker on one of the 13 main-board locations and then activating same-type structures from your tableau. Generate turns revolve around reclaiming pieces, refining oil, rolling energy dice, and then dealing with pollution based on the highest die rolled. That means the game's tempo pressure is not abstract. It is built directly into the turn rhythm.

Instead of centering military escalation, Energy Empire centers modernization, pollution, and global impacts. Occupied spaces can still be used, but only if you build the tallest stack by placing energy beneath your worker. Players may even overstack on purpose to make the location more expensive for later players. This is a very instructive move for a solo-first designer: the game preserves tension without requiring direct hostility.

That change matters because it shows one of the clearest design forks in this genre:

| Fork | Strong version | Weak version |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Space tension | Choosing an action feels costly because timing matters | Spaces are occupied, but players can shrug and do something nearly equivalent |
| Tableau growth | New cards change future priorities and combos | Cards mostly drip extra resources without changing decisions |
| Interaction | Players feel the table through denial, races, pressure, and competition | Everyone mostly solves their own puzzle with minimal consequences |
| Scoring | Points reward a coherent engine and timing decisions | Points come from many disconnected sources that flatten identity |

If you want to design in the neighborhood of these games, you are not trying to clone their themes, iconography, or exact action menus. You are trying to learn why their turns feel consequential. The key questions are:

1. Why does a placement matter right now?
2. Why does building this card matter later?
3. Why does the table care what I just did?
4. Why does my engine feel like *my* engine rather than a pile of generic efficiency pieces?

> [!TIP]
> When studying benchmark games, do not ask "What actions do they have?" first. Ask "What pressures do their actions create?"

A good medium-weight design usually gives the player a feeling of competence without full certainty. They can read the board, imagine a plan, and see opportunities. But they cannot do everything. Their worker count, resource capacity, card timing, and turn order create productive friction. That friction is what makes the genre satisfying.

For your own project, define the target feel before you define the whole system. Maybe you want:

- industrial growth under environmental pressure
- rebuilding a ruined colony through civic investment
- magical guilds competing for influence while building institutions
- solo survival plus expansion in a frontier region

The key is that the worker placement layer should create tactical timing pressure, while the tableau layer should create strategic identity. Energy Empire's rulebook reinforces this by restricting phase-two structure activations to the same binder type as the board location you visited that turn, while still allowing your Nation card on any Work turn. That is a crisp example of systems having different jobs while still talking to each other. If both layers are doing the same job, the game will feel flatter than its complexity suggests.

## Checklist
- Write a one-sentence design takeaway from the original Manhattan Project.
- Write a one-sentence design takeaway from Energy Empire.
- Name one kind of interaction you want in your design and one kind you want to avoid.
- Define the emotional promise of your game in one sentence: "Players will feel like..."

## Reflection
- Which shift appeals to you more for your own game: the sharper confrontation of The Manhattan Project or the broader pressure system of Energy Empire, and why?
- If your future design became memorable for one thing only, what would you most want players to remember: the action tension, the engine growth, the solo challenge, or the thematic arc?

## Quiz
### Question 1
Why are The Manhattan Project and Energy Empire especially useful benchmark games for this course?
- [ ] Because they are the only worker placement games with personal boards
- [x] Because they combine contested action selection with player-built engines that change future turns
- [ ] Because they avoid all interaction and focus only on efficiency
- [ ] Because their themes can be safely copied into new designs
Explanation: The important lesson is the combination of pressure on the board and growth in the tableau, not their exact themes or components.

### Question 2
What is the healthiest way to study a benchmark game?
- [ ] Copy its action spaces and rename them
- [ ] Copy its scoring numbers because they are already balanced
- [x] Identify the pressures, tradeoffs, and emotional texture the systems create
- [ ] Ignore the game entirely once you know the theme does not match yours
Explanation: Studying the structural reasons a game feels good is much more valuable than copying visible content.

### Question 3
What is one important design lesson in Energy Empire's occupied-space system?
- [ ] Blocking should always be total and absolute
- [ ] Every action should be free if another player already used it
- [ ] Players should never be punished for taking contested actions
- [x] You can preserve tension by making contested actions more expensive rather than fully unavailable
Explanation: Energy Empire shows a softer but still meaningful form of worker-placement pressure that is especially interesting for solo-first or lower-conflict designs.
