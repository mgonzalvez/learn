## Objectives
- Design a tableau that changes player priorities instead of only paying passive income.
- Understand how engine-building, conversion chains, and card identity reinforce each other.
- Draft the main tableau lanes for your own game.

## Lesson
In this genre, the tableau is where your game stops being "the board everyone shares" and starts becoming "the machine I am building." That is why weak tableau design is so damaging. If the tableau only adds small bonuses, the game feels like a worker placement game with extra paperwork.

The Manhattan Project family provides a clear model: the main board gives access, but your personal structures increasingly shape what your turns are about. Energy Empire pushes this further by giving players multiple structure families and a broader civic-industrial feeling. Luke Laurie even describes the game as having three board regions that match three card types, meaning the tableau is not one amorphous stack of upgrades. It has lanes.

That idea is extremely valuable. Good tableaus often separate into meaningful lanes such as:

- production
- conversion
- mitigation
- scoring
- tempo
- card cycling or selection

You do not need six lanes. In fact, for a first prototype, three clear lanes is often plenty.

### The three healthiest jobs for tableau cards

### 1. Enable

These cards unlock or improve what you can do.

Examples:

- build in a cheaper way
- store more of a resource
- ignore a placement tax once per round
- treat one worker type as another for a specific action

### 2. Convert

These cards turn your raw economy into better outputs.

Examples:

- coal into power
- berries into points
- labor into research
- pollution into a late-game discount if cleaned efficiently

### 3. Reward

These cards pay you for building around a plan.

Examples:

- points for science tags
- endgame points for diversified districts
- bonuses for clean energy infrastructure
- tempo rewards for completing contracts

If a card does not enable, convert, or reward in a meaningful way, it is at risk of being filler.

Underwater Cities is a useful comparison because it shows how cards can feel fused to action timing rather than appended to it. Everdell is useful because players love the feeling of building synergies and card families into a city with identity. The lesson is not "put lots of combo text on cards." The lesson is that cards should make the next turns feel different.

### Avoiding bland tableau growth

Here are some common weak patterns:

- cards that all just add one more resource
- cards that are only better versions of earlier cards
- cards that score in isolation without asking anything of the system
- cards that create complexity without changing player incentives

Here are stronger alternatives:

- cards that make crowded spaces attractive
- cards that reward specializing *or* deliberately diversifying
- cards that transform a liability into an engine
- cards that make the timing of retrieval, cleanup, or storage genuinely interesting

For a Manhattan Project / Energy Empire inspired design, one especially strong pattern is the **conversion arc**:

1. workers access resources or infrastructure
2. tableau cards make those inputs more flexible
3. the player converts them into bigger strategic outputs
4. scoring rewards the line they built around

That arc feels good because each layer matters. The board gathers. The tableau shapes. The scoring validates.

> [!TIP]
> A tableau should not merely answer "What do I get?" It should answer "What kind of player am I becoming in this game?"

When drafting your own tableau, try this first-pass structure:

| Lane | Design purpose | Example card idea |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Infrastructure | Makes core actions smoother | "Once per round, pay one less energy when using an occupied industrial space." |
| Conversion | Turns base resources into premium outputs | "Spend 2 metal and 1 worker to generate 1 reactor part and 1 research." |
| Policy / scoring | Rewards a direction | "Score 2 points each time you build a civic card after you have 3 clean-energy tags." |

Notice how each lane speaks to a different design need.

If you are designing solo first, this is even more important. Solo players need a clear feeling of engine authorship. If the bot supplies all the drama and the tableau feels generic, the solo game becomes procedural instead of satisfying.

## Checklist
- Draft 12 to 18 first-pass cards across 3 tableau lanes.
- Label each card as enable, convert, or reward.
- Remove any card that only adds a passive trickle without changing decisions.
- Identify one liability in your game that a clever tableau card could transform into opportunity.

## Reflection
- What kind of engine sounds more exciting for you to build right now: a clean streamlined specialist engine or a flexible cross-lane combo engine?
- If your tableau became famous for one quality, would you rather it feel elegant, explosive, or deeply adaptable?

## Quiz
### Question 1
What is the main risk of a weak tableau in this genre?
- [ ] It makes workers too important
- [ ] It causes the board to become more interactive
- [x] It adds maintenance and bonuses without meaningfully changing player priorities
- [ ] It creates too many scoring opportunities
Explanation: A weak tableau creates more overhead than identity. Strong tableaus reshape choices and future turns.

### Question 2
Which trio captures three especially useful jobs for tableau cards?
- [ ] Decorate, randomize, and replace
- [ ] Gather, block, and end the game
- [x] Enable, convert, and reward
- [ ] Teach, simplify, and automate
Explanation: Those three jobs keep cards focused on system function instead of filler text.

### Question 3
Why is lane structure helpful when designing a tableau?
- [ ] It guarantees perfect balance
- [ ] It prevents players from building combos
- [ ] It makes every card independent of the board
- [x] It gives cards clearer roles and helps the engine develop identity instead of feeling like one big pile
Explanation: Distinct lanes make the tableau easier to develop, read, and balance.

### Question 4
What is a strong sign that a card is worth keeping?
- [ ] It adds text even if players ignore it
- [x] It changes what the player wants to do with future actions or resources
- [ ] It only works in one niche corner case
- [ ] It is the most complicated card in the deck
Explanation: Strong cards change incentives, not just numbers.
