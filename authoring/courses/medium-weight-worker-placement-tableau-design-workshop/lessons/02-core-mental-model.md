## Objectives
- Recognize recurring structural strengths across standout worker placement and tableau-building games.
- Learn what Viticulture, Everdell, Underwater Cities, and Paladins each contribute to the design conversation.
- Build a shortlist of design patterns worth borrowing in principle, not by imitation.

## Lesson
Once you move beyond the Manhattan Project family, a useful pattern appears: the best medium-weight games do not merely pile mechanisms together. They assign clear jobs to each mechanism.

Viticulture uses seasonal structure and visitor cards to make timing feel gentle but meaningful. The board is divided by season, so a worker is not simply choosing *what* to do, but *when* in the year to do it. The cards do not exist merely to add variety; they give players tactical pivots and alternative lines. This teaches an important lesson: your tableau or card layer does not always need to be the primary engine. It can be the flexibility layer that helps the core placement system stay fresh.

Everdell makes the tableau emotionally central. Your city grows card by card, and the worker placement system helps feed, accelerate, and frame those card decisions. The result is a game where players care deeply about combos, but still feel the board as a race for key resources and locations. This is instructive if you want a more welcoming presentation: a game can feel cozy or inviting while still rewarding tight sequencing.

Underwater Cities is especially valuable because it links action selection to card timing rather than treating them as separate lanes. Matching the color of the action and the card matters. That means the tableau system is not just an engine that pays later; it is entangled with the action system itself. This is one of the best models to study if you want each turn to feel integrated rather than modular.

Paladins of the West Kingdom pushes toward the heavier side of medium-weight, but it is extremely useful for understanding role specialization and internal tension. Different worker colors, the round paladin, and a dense personal board combine to make each round feel like a constrained puzzle with long-term consequences. It proves that a personal tableau can be far more than passive bonuses; it can be the real geography of the design.

Across these games, a few common strengths keep showing up:

### Pattern 1: The action spaces are not generic

Strong action spaces are not interchangeable. They create a real question:

- now or later?
- early or contested?
- broad gain or narrow setup?
- public pressure or private efficiency?

If your action row could be rearranged without changing the game much, it is probably too flat.

### Pattern 2: The tableau changes decision quality, not only income

A weak tableau says, "Gain one extra wood every round."

A stronger tableau says, "This card makes a previously awkward path finally coherent," or "This card changes what resources I value," or "This card turns a blocked space into an opportunity."

That difference is huge. The best tableaus do not merely pay. They reshape what the player wants.

### Pattern 3: Constraint is part of the fun

Medium-weight games are not fun because players can do everything. They are fun because the system withholds enough capacity that players must prioritize. Different worker types, hand limits, city caps, storage limits, round structure, seasonal splits, and action matching are all ways of creating meaningful constraint.

### Pattern 4: Interaction can be indirect and still strong

Not every good worker placement game needs attack actions. Racing for spaces, shared markets, card denial, timing windows, achievements, and contested tempo all produce genuine interaction. For solo-first design, this matters a lot. You want meaningful pressure without requiring cruelty as the main dramatic tool.

> [!TIP]
> When comparing benchmark games, ask: what is the *minimum* system that creates each game's signature feeling?

That question stops you from overbuilding. For example:

- Viticulture's signature is not "lots of cards." It is seasonal tempo plus visitor opportunism.
- Everdell's signature is not "cute art." It is card-combo joy plus a city cap that forces hard choices.
- Underwater Cities' signature is not "science fiction." It is integrated action-card matching.
- Paladins' signature is not simply "worker colors." It is layered constraint plus focused round identity.

For your design, you do not need all of these strengths at once. In fact, trying to include every admired feature usually creates sludge. Choose two or three signature pressures and build around them.

## Checklist
- Write down the one most useful lesson you want to borrow from Viticulture, Everdell, Underwater Cities, and Paladins.
- Circle one pattern you want to emphasize in your game: timing, combo growth, action-card integration, or specialized workers.
- List two kinds of constraint your game will use on purpose.
- Name one "cool feature" you are now less tempted to include because it would dilute the design.

## Reflection
- Which comparison game feels closest to your design instincts right now, and what does that reveal about the experience you want to build?
- If your game could be praised for one structural virtue, what would you rather hear: "every placement mattered" or "my tableau felt amazing to build"?

## Quiz
### Question 1
What is a shared strength across the benchmark comparison games in this lesson?
- [ ] They all use direct attack as the main source of tension
- [ ] They all make the tableau more important than action selection
- [x] They give different jobs to their systems instead of letting every mechanism do everything
- [ ] They avoid constraints so that players feel free every turn
Explanation: Good medium-weight designs tend to assign distinct roles to action spaces, cards, timing structure, and scoring rather than stacking unfocused subsystems.

### Question 2
What is a stronger sign of a healthy tableau?
- [ ] It gives small passive bonuses but rarely changes your priorities
- [ ] It increases upkeep and bookkeeping every round
- [x] It changes what lines become attractive and how you value future turns
- [ ] It replaces the need for a shared board entirely
Explanation: The best tableaus reshape planning and identity, not just resource drip.

### Question 3
Why is indirect interaction important for a solo-first designer to study?
- [ ] Because solo games should remove all competition from the system
- [x] Because pressure can come from races, denial, tempo, and shared markets without relying on harsh conflict
- [ ] Because direct interaction is always bad design
- [ ] Because solo modes only work if the multiplayer game has no interaction
Explanation: Indirect interaction often adapts more gracefully to solo systems and broader player preferences while still keeping the game alive and contested.
